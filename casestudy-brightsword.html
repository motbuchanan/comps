Bright Sword Artisan

The sighting

Coworker's side business: handmade leather goods, hand-turned pens, knives. Sells at craft shows around Massillon. Facebook only. No website, no booking, custom work advertised on a paper sign.

Kestrel entry, 30 seconds:

Case study · step 2 of 6

Recon

Ten photos from his Facebook. No conversation needed yet.

Extracted from the photos alone

  • Full price list read straight off his booth sign: bookmarks $5, straps $5, minimalist wallets $15, traveler's journals $25, clutches $70, pens/knives/bags per piece
  • Tagline material: his table sign says "All items are handmade in Massillon, Ohio"
  • The best line of copy he owns, from his own pricing sheet: "If you can think of it, we can make it"
  • Payments: cash, PayPal, Cash App (also on the sign)
  • Logo: paid for, personal connection, locked. So it gets featured, never redrawn.

Still missing

Phone number, real show schedule, whether he owns a domain. All three get answered in one conversation, and none block the demo: samples are labeled as samples.

Case study · step 3 of 6

Archetype match

The decision that picks everything else.

Roamer

The schedule is the product: people who love his booth have no way to find the next one. Skeleton: food truck file, maker theme, one word in CONFIG. Labels swapped: Menu → Catalog, Where we're parked → Find the booth.

Ticket-size check

$5–70 goods alone would NOT justify a $900 site. What tips it: knives and custom commissions, where one order can be hundreds. The site's real job is turning "if you can think of it, we can make it" from a paper sign into an order pipeline.

If the site's too big a bite, the small doors fit him perfectly: a QR table card pointing at a one-pager, or a price-board redesign to replace the printer-paper signs.

Case study · step 6 of 6

The pitch

Warm target version. Break room, two minutes.

The opener

"Hey, side-hustle to side-hustle: I've been building websites. I made a mockup of what Bright Sword could look like online. Thirty seconds, want to see?"

Phone out. Scroll slowly. Stop talking at the catalog.

The one question

"Where do your custom orders come from right now, and how do people find your next show?"

Both answers are the sale. If commissions come from word of mouth and shows are announced nowhere, the site is the answer to problems he already feels.

Deal shapes, in order

  • Full build, $900: this comp plus a custom-order request form and his real schedule, on a platform he owns, handed off. Friends-and-family price is the same price.
  • Small door: QR table card + one-pager if $900 is too big right now. Gets his booth traffic capturing itself.
  • Trade caution: he makes beautiful things, and a wallet is not a website. If a trade comes up, both sides get valued in dollars, in writing, like the rules say.

Then, in Kestrel

Stage to Pitched, follow-up date set, demo link saved on the card, and whatever he said about custom orders goes in Notes. Comp stays up a week either way, and either way it just became the maker-archetype template.
